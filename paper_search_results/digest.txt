### liu2021_rigorous_quantum_speedup | 2021 (arXiv 2020, Nature Physics 2021)
TITLE: A rigorous and robust quantum speed-up in supervised machine learning
ONE_LINE: Constructs a discrete-logarithm-based classification task that is provably hard for any classical learner (no better than random guessing under the DLP hardness assumption) yet is efficiently and noise-robustly learnable by a support vector machine using a quantum-kernel feature map, giving the first end-to-end provable quantum speed-up in supervised learning with only classical data access.
QUBITS: n = ceil(log2 p) qubits, where p is a large prime (chosen large enough that DLP is classically intractable; in practice p would be hundreds of bits, so n is in the hundreds-to-thousands of qubits and far exceeds 20). No simulation or hardware experiment was run, so no concrete qubit count <=20 or <=30 is demonstrated. The speed-up regime requires fault-tolerant scale qubits (DLP-hardness needs n large); it is NOT instantiated at the 20-30 qubit simulable scale.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Yes — this is the canonical example of a task where classical methods provably fail and a quantum method provably wins, but with critical caveats for the 20+-qubit-simulation hypothesis. Strength: the classical hardness is a PROVEN reduction (no efficient classical learner beats 1/2 + 1/poly(n)) conditional on the widely-believed but UNPROVEN hardness of discrete log; it is robust to dequantization because the kernel itself is DLP-hard to estimate (Lemma 12) and uses only classical data access (immune to the Tang-style quantum-inspired attacks that killed amplitude-encoding speed-ups). The quantum side is also proven (Theorem 22), including noise robustness to finite-shot sampling error. KEY CAVEAT for simulability: the separation only exists asymptotically and requires n = ceil(log2 p) qubits with p large enough that DLP is hard — i.e., hundreds of bits/qubits and fault-tolerant Shor execution. At <=30 (or any classically simulable) qubit count, the discrete log is trivially solvable classically, so there is NO advantage in the simulable regime; one cannot demonstrate this speed-up with a 20-30 qubit simulation. Additional caveats: the task is artificial/not practically motivated (authors explicitly say so), the feature map embeds Shor's algorithm, the conditional hardness assumption (DLP) could fail, and DLP being easy quantumly means the kernel method is not exploiting anything beyond Shor. No empirical experiment is reported.
DATASETS: A purpose-engineered synthetic family of datasets parameterized by a prime p and generator g of Z_p*. Data space X = Z_p* (n = ceil(log2 p)-bit integers), uniform distribution; concept secret key s in Z_p*; binary labels {-1,+1} split 50/50. Training set size m = poly(n) (analysis uses m = n^c with t = c/3). No fixed numeric p, n, or m instantiated; no real-world data. Labeled examples can be generated classically in poly time despite labels being hard to compute (sample random y, label by interval, return (g^y, label)).
CODE: none (purely theoretical paper; no repository, no simulation, no Qiskit/PennyLane/TFQ implementation reported)
RELEVANCE: High relevance as the foundational theory reference for "classical-hard / quantum-easy" supervised learning, but LOW relevance as direct support for the specific hypothesis of demonstrating advantage via simulation with 20-30 qubits. It rigorously establishes that such separating tasks EXIST (under DLP hardness) and that quantum kernel methods can be provably advantageous and noise-robust; however, the advantage is asymptotic and only manifests when the discrete log is classically intractable, which requires far more than 30 qubits and fault-tolerance, making the separation impossible to exhibit in a small classically-simulable regime. Essential to cite for framing, motivation, and the QKE methodology, but its construction cannot itself be the simulable demonstration.

### huang2021_power_of_data | 2021
TITLE: Power of data in quantum machine learning
ONE_LINE: Introduces a geometric-difference test (g) and rigorous prediction-error bounds showing that data can let classical ML match quantum models even on classically-hard-to-compute data, and proposes the projected quantum kernel which empirically beats all tested classical models on engineered datasets up to 30 qubits and gives a provable fault-tolerant speedup on a discrete-log task.
QUBITS: Simulation only (no hardware); engineered-dataset experiments scale up to 30 qubits (system size n in plots runs to ~30); original quantum kernel reported only up to n=28 due to simulation cost and poor performance. Well above the 20-qubit threshold of interest.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Yes, but with important nuance. (1) Empirical: on engineered datasets the projected quantum kernel beats ALL tested classical models (including kernel-free ones like random forests) by >20% accuracy at up to 30 simulated qubits when g is large (E3 embedding) -- the first empirical demonstration of such a large quantum/classical ML prediction separation, robust to moderate noise and stable across classical methods. Caveat: this is a deliberately adversarially-engineered label function chosen to saturate s_C=g^2 s_Q, not a naturally occurring task; the advantage shrinks as N grows ("power of data"), and g is minimized only over a finite classical-model suite, so a stronger/unconsidered classical model could narrow the gap. (2) Provable (not just empirical): the discrete-log projected-kernel result is a rigorous separation, but it relies on Shor's algorithm for the feature map and the hardness of discrete log -- i.e. it lives in the fault-tolerant regime and is not a 30-qubit-simulable advantage. (3) The paper itself emphasizes the negative direction: data elevates classical ML so that naive quantum kernels on classically-hard data usually show NO advantage (small g), and warns that claims of advantage require benchmarking against classical approximations, not just classical ML. So the genuine simulable (<=30 qubit) win is empirical and on engineered data; the only fully rigorous win needs fault-tolerance.
DATASETS: Fashion-MNIST images (shirt vs dress) reduced via PCA to n-dimensional vectors (n = number of qubits), normalized, subsampled to 800 points; N=600 (and N=100/600) used for evaluation. Quantum labels are expectation values of a local observable after a random-coupling 1D-Heisenberg Trotter QNN. Engineered/adversarial datasets are synthesized by solving a generalized eigenvalue problem to saturate the geometric inequality s_C = g(K_C||K_PQ)^2; converted to a binary classification task via median thresholding. A separate synthetic discrete-logarithm dataset (Definition 1) is used for the provable speedup.
CODE: Built on TensorFlow and TensorFlow-Quantum (Broughton et al. 2020) with scikit-learn for PCA/SVM and Neural Tangents referenced; no explicit public repository link given in the paper text.
RELEVANCE: high -- Directly central to the hypothesis: it is the canonical paper formalizing WHEN classical ML struggles and quantum wins, provides the geometric-difference diagnostic, demonstrates an empirical quantum-over-classical ML separation via simulation at exactly the 20-30 qubit scale of interest, and supplies a concrete recipe (projected quantum kernel + engineered datasets) for constructing such tasks, while also delivering the key cautionary caveats (data dequantizes naive quantum advantage; rigorous separation needs fault tolerance).

### havlicek2019_quantum_feature_spaces | 2019 (Nature 567:209-212; arXiv:1804.11326, 2018)
TITLE: Supervised learning with quantum enhanced feature spaces
ONE_LINE: Introduces two SVM-style classifiers that encode classical data into a quantum Hilbert space via a conjecturally-hard-to-simulate feature map—a variational quantum classifier and a quantum kernel estimator—and demonstrates them on a 2-qubit superconducting processor with up to 100% classification accuracy.
QUBITS: Experiment: 2 qubits (Q0, Q1) on a 5-qubit superconducting transmon device; only n=d=2 used. Theory/algorithms stated for general n qubits with feature space dimension 4^n, but no simulation or hardware runs at >=20 qubits are reported. All concrete results are at n=2.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Only partially and conjecturally. The paper does NOT empirically demonstrate a task where a classical method fails and quantum wins: the synthetic data is explicitly engineered to be 100% separable by the chosen quantum feature map, and no classical baseline is shown to fail on it (verification of hardware, not advantage, is the stated goal). The claimed route to advantage rests entirely on a CONJECTURE that the kernel K=|<Phi(x)|Phi(z)>|^2 of the two-basis-change feature map is hard to estimate classically up to additive polynomial error, motivated by an analogy to the hidden-shift / bent-function problem (Roetteler) with a query-complexity (oracle) separation—not a proven separation for this kernel. The authors explicitly note caveats: (i) the single-layer/IQP version (Bremner et al.) is #P-hard only for multiplicative error, but its kernel is efficiently estimable classically to additive error via uniform sampling (R~eps^-2)—i.e., a dequantization for the simpler map; (ii) product-state and shallow maps are classically simulable; (iii) noisy quantum devices cannot give multiplicative-error estimates either. They state that if the conjecture could be proven it 'would establish a valuable step in rigorously establishing a quantum advantage,' and if it fails the circuit family must be made harder. Net: advantage is conjectured, not proven or empirically shown; and the regime studied (2 qubits) is trivially classically simulable.
DATASETS: Artificially engineered 2-D synthetic data on Omega=(0,2pi]^2, constructed to be perfectly separable by the chosen feature map. Labels generated via a random V in SU(4) and parity f=Z1Z2: m=+1 if <Phi|V^dag f V|Phi> >= Delta, m=-1 if <= -Delta, with separation gap Delta=0.3. Training and test sets each have 20 points per label (40 total). Three datasets (Set I, II, III) were studied; no real-world data used.
CODE: None provided in the paper (no repository link). Methods later became QSVM / ZZFeatureMap in IBM Qiskit (qiskit-machine-learning), but no code is referenced in the manuscript.
RELEVANCE: High. This is a foundational, widely-cited core paper that directly frames the hypothesis: it proposes encoding classical data into a high-dimensional quantum feature/Hilbert space and argues a quantum advantage can arise specifically when the kernel is classically hard to estimate. It clearly delineates WHEN classical methods would and would not struggle (product/shallow maps = no advantage; conjecturally-hard entangling maps = potential advantage). Caveats for the hypothesis: the advantage is only conjectured (with an explicit dequantization of the simpler IQP variant), the demonstration uses only 2 qubits (not the >=20-qubit simulable regime targeted), and the data is engineered rather than a natural task where classical ML provably fails.

### peters2021_projected_kernel_hardware | 2021
TITLE: Machine learning of high dimensional data on a noisy quantum processor
ONE_LINE: Demonstrates a fidelity quantum-kernel SVM on Google's Sycamore superconducting processor that classifies real 67-dimensional supernova data with no dimensionality reduction, achieving test accuracy competitive with noiseless simulation and a classical RBF-SVM despite ~30% circuit fidelity.
QUBITS: Hardware: n in {10, 14, 17} qubits on Sycamore (23 active qubits, max 19 assignable with their path connectivity). Noiseless simulation used the same up to 17 qubits. Type 1 ablation simulated 2-17 qubits. Does NOT use >=20 qubits in this paper's own experiments (max 17); the authors cite a companion result (Huang et al., "Power of data", ref [4]) numerically validated on up to 30 qubits in TensorFlow Quantum, but that is not this paper's own work.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: No genuine quantum advantage is demonstrated or claimed. The paper explicitly states the circuits "are not candidates for demonstrating quantum advantage" because the Type 2 ansatz can be made arbitrarily shallow in qubit number (O(d/n) depth) and the c2->0 / small-angle limits are classically/trivially simulable. The reported quantum accuracy is at best comparable to (not exceeding) the classical RBF-SVM baseline; both fail to reach 100% accuracy, and the abstract only frames the supernova task as one "for which competitive classical classifiers fail to achieve 100% accuracy" - not one where classical methods fail and quantum wins. The main result is feasibility (handling real high-dim data without dimensionality reduction and robustness to noise), not separation. Any advantage is purely conjectured/aspirational: the conclusion names finding a "natural" dataset with classically-hard correlations (e.g., quantum many-body, quantum-sensor data) as the open problem, and defers the proposed advantage to the cited Huang et al. engineered-dataset/30-qubit TFQ work. Caveats: the relevant <=30-qubit simulable regime here is, by the authors' own admission, classically tractable; no dequantization is discussed but the easy-simulability of the shallow ansatz is the key limiting caveat. NOTE: despite the filename "projected_kernel_hardware," this paper uses a standard fidelity kernel |<phi|phi>|^2, NOT a projected quantum kernel.
DATASETS: PLAsTiCC (Photometric LSST Astronomical Time-series Classification Challenge) simulated supernova data from the Vera C. Rubin Observatory; binary classification of the two most-represented classes 42 (type II SN) vs 90 (type Ia SN). Time series engineered into 67-dimensional feature vectors with NO dimensionality reduction. Train/test split m=210, v=70 (balanced); dataset selected from a 1000x1000 simulated kernel matrix to match mean cross-validation performance. 5000 repetitions/circuit, ~1.83x10^8 experiments per qubit count.
CODE: No dedicated repository released. Built on Cirq (quantum simulation/execution) and scikit-learn (SVM); references TensorFlow Quantum (TFQ). Hardware accessed via Google Quantum Computing Service (Sycamore). Graph routines via NetworkX.
RELEVANCE: medium - It is a landmark NISQ-hardware quantum-kernel demonstration on real, high-dimensional (67-dim) data and is methodologically central to the quantum-kernel-SVM literature the hypothesis targets. However, relevance to the specific hypothesis (classical struggles, quantum via 20+ qubit simulation excels) is only partial: it uses at most 17 qubits (not >=20), runs primarily on hardware rather than simulation, deliberately uses a classically-simulable ansatz, and shows quantum merely comparable to (not beating) classical - explicitly disclaiming quantum advantage. Useful as a baseline/feasibility anchor and for circuit-design and noise-robustness lessons, and it points (via ref [4]) to the 30-qubit engineered-advantage work that more directly fits the hypothesis.

### abbas2021_power_qnn | 2021 (arXiv 2020, Nature Computational Science 2021)
TITLE: The power of quantum neural networks
ONE_LINE: Introduces the Fisher-information-based "effective dimension" as a data-aware capacity measure, proves a generalisation bound for it, and uses it (plus the Fisher spectrum) to argue that a QNN with a hard-to-simulate feature map achieves higher effective dimension and faster/more resilient training than comparable classical neural networks.
QUBITS: Simulation: S = s_in qubits with s_in in {4,6,8,10}, i.e. 4-10 qubits (all <20). Hardware: 4 qubits used on the 27-qubit ibmq_montreal device. No experiment reaches 20+ qubits.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Only weakly and indirectly. The paper does NOT exhibit a concrete classification task where classical ML fails and quantum wins on accuracy; on Iris all models train, the QNN just reaches lower loss faster. The "advantage" is a capacity/trainability claim: the QNN has higher effective dimension and a non-degenerate, more uniform Fisher spectrum than matched classical nets, which empirically correlates with faster training and barren-plateau resilience. Key caveats: (1) the claimed power hinges entirely on the feature map being hard to classically simulate, which is only CONJECTURED (for depth>=2), not proven, and the easy (simulable) quantum model loses the advantage; (2) higher effective dimension is a double-edged proxy (more capacity can hurt generalisation), and the bound is a uniform-convergence upper bound, not a separation; (3) experiments use only 4-10 qubits, so nothing demonstrates the 20+ qubit regime; (4) no comparison to kernels or dequantization, no proof of any quantum speedup/learning separation. So: empirical capacity/trainability advantage at small scale, advantage conjectural, no proven task where classical provably fails.
DATASETS: Iris (first two classes, 100 points) for the training experiments; scikit-learn make_blobs confusion sets (1000 points, increasing label randomisation 0-50%) to validate the effective dimension as a generalisation proxy. Capacity/Fisher-spectrum experiments are synthetic: parameters theta sampled uniformly on [-1,1]^d, Fisher computed 100 times per model with a standard Gaussian data prior. Configs: d<=100, s_in in {4,6,8,10}, s_out=2.
CODE: No dedicated repository given; implemented in Qiskit (IBM Quantum Experience), classical nets standard. scikit-learn used for make_blobs/Iris.
RELEVANCE: High. It is a foundational, frequently-cited theoretical/empirical argument that a well-designed QNN can be "more powerful" (higher effective dimension, better-conditioned Fisher landscape, barren-plateau resilience) than comparable classical nets, directly addressing the hypothesis. Relevance is tempered for the specific "20+ qubit simulation where classical struggles" angle because experiments top out at 10 simulated / 4 hardware qubits and the advantage is capacity-based and conjectural rather than a demonstrated task-level classical failure.

### kubler2021_inductive_bias_quantum_kernels | 2021
TITLE: The Inductive Bias of Quantum Kernels
ONE_LINE: A spectral analysis of quantum kernels showing that an expressive (exponentially large) quantum feature space destroys generalization, so a quantum advantage in kernel learning requires encoding a problem-specific inductive bias that is classically hard to encode — which the authors argue is plausible only for quantum-generated data, not generic classical datasets.
QUBITS: Simulation only (full state vector), d = 1 to 7 qubits. No quantum hardware. No experiments at >=20 qubits; theoretical claims are asymptotic in d but empirical demonstration is limited to <=7 qubits.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Partial and heavily caveated. The paper does construct a task where the classical RBF kernel (and the unbiased quantum kernel) fail to generalize while the correctly biased quantum kernel succeeds — but this is by construction, not a demonstration of generic classical hardness. The 'advantage' rests on an inductive-bias argument: the eigenfunctions φ_1,2,3 of the projected kernel are *conjectured* (citing Bremner et al.-type IQP hardness, ref [48]) to be exponentially hard to compute classically, so a classical model cannot bias toward them; this is conjectured, not proven. Two strong caveats undercut a usable advantage: (1) the favorable eigenvalues are O(2^-d), so Theorem 2a implies estimating the kernel from measurements requires exponentially many shots — a kernel-analogue of barren plateaus — so the simulated advantage is unlikely to survive measurement-based evaluation on hardware; (2) the bias must be known a priori (one must know f* = Tr[ρ̃^V_1(x)M*]), which is unrealistic for real classical datasets. The empirical win is shown only at <=7 qubits (simulable). The genuinely proven classical-hardness example they cite is the discrete-log task of Liu et al. (separate paper), which needs a fault-tolerant computer. Net message of the paper itself is 'sobering': they conclude there is no indication QML will substantially improve supervised learning on classical datasets; advantage is plausible mainly for quantum-generated data. No dequantization is performed, but the centering trick they note means the only classically-encodable bias (the constant function) is the one a classical model can already exploit.
DATASETS: Synthetic/engineered only. Data drawn from the d-dimensional uniform distribution on [0,2π]^d, N=200 samples (2/3 train, 1/3 test). Labels y = c·f*(x) + ε with f*(x)=Tr[ρ̃^V_1(x)σ_z] generated by measuring observable M*=σ_z on the first qubit after a fixed random unitary V, with Gaussian noise Var[ε]=10^-4 and c set so Var[f(X)]=1. No real/classical datasets used.
CODE: Yes — https://github.com/jmkuebler/quantumbias (builds on standard open-source packages, refs [50,51]; full-state simulation).
RELEVANCE: High. The paper directly addresses the hypothesis (tasks where classical struggles and quantum wins) and provides a rigorous spectral framework for *when* such an advantage can exist. It is a crucial cautionary/conditions paper: it shows the advantage requires a classically-inexpressible inductive bias, demonstrates it only in a constructed synthetic task at <=7 simulated qubits, and identifies the exponential-measurement obstruction. Essential for critically scoping any '20+ qubit simulation' claim of advantage on classical data.

### schuld2021_models_are_kernels | 2021
TITLE: Supervised quantum machine learning models are kernel methods
ONE_LINE: A technical manuscript proving that essentially all supervised quantum models (data-encoding circuit + measurement) are mathematically equivalent to classical kernel methods whose kernel is computed on a quantum computer, so the data-encoding strategy (which fixes the kernel) is the only ingredient that can possibly separate quantum from classical ML.
QUBITS: Theoretical: encodings use O(n) qubits (basis, amplitude, rotation) or O(rn) (repeated amplitude); general feature space is 2^n x 2^n. No specific hardware runs and no simulations at fixed large qubit counts; toy illustrations are single-qubit (n=1) or few-qubit. No experiments at >=20 qubits.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Does NOT empirically demonstrate any task where classical methods fail and a simulable quantum method wins. The paper's thesis actually constrains quantum advantage: because every quantum model is a classical kernel method, the ONLY possible separation is a quantum kernel that is classically hard to compute. Schuld explicitly states the existence of such a kernel relies on a conjectured classically-hard computation (ref. [11], Liu-Arunachalam-Temme) and stresses "it is still unclear whether any useful applications turn out to be enabled solely by quantum computers" and "the question of quantum kernels that are actually useful for every-day problems is still wide open." Any advantage is therefore conjectured/conditional, not proven or empirically shown here. Critically, many highlighted encodings dequantize trivially: amplitude encoding gives |x^dagger x'|^2 (essentially a linear kernel), rotation gives a classical cosine kernel, and coherent-state encoding gives the classical Gaussian kernel - all efficiently classically computable, hence NO advantage. The quantum-training speedup (O(M)) requires fault-tolerant hardware and strong data-loading-oracle (QRAM) assumptions, and is a runtime claim, not a learning-capability claim. Caveat for the hypothesis: separation would require a kernel that is hard to simulate classically, which generally precludes the <=30-qubit exact-simulation regime; so this paper offers no support for a simulable (<=30 qubit) quantum-wins task and arguably argues against expecting one.
DATASETS: None used for empirical evaluation. The paper is theoretical; illustrative figures (Figs. 6-8) plot kernel functions for a single-qubit Pauli-X / rotation embedding over toy 1D-2D synthetic inputs (e.g., equidistant points on an interval, x in R^2 with one point fixed), generated via PennyLane simulations. A referenced (not included) PennyLane demo uses ~100 data samples with 10-20 parameters for timing.
CODE: No paper-specific repository. Uses PennyLane (Bergholm et al. 2018, ref. [23]) for kernel-plot simulations; references a public PennyLane demo on kernel-based training (pennylane.ai/qml/demos/tutorial_kernel_based_training.html).
RELEVANCE: High - this is a foundational/conceptual anchor for the hypothesis, but as a cautionary one. It rigorously frames WHERE any quantum-vs-classical ML separation can come from (only a classically-hard-to-compute data-encoding kernel) and makes clear that such separations are conjectural and that common encodings are dequantizable. It is essential context for critically scoping claims of "quantum excels where classical struggles," though it provides no positive evidence of a simulable quantum advantage.

### glick2021_covariant_kernels | 2021 (arXiv 2105.03406; v2 Mar 2022)
TITLE: Covariant quantum kernels for data with group structure
ONE_LINE: Introduces covariant quantum kernels for data with group structure—built from a unitary group representation and an optimizable fiducial state—and demonstrates them on a 27-qubit superconducting processor for a coset-labeling classification task.
QUBITS: 27 qubits on hardware (ibmq_kolkata, heavy-hexagon), G=SU(2)^⊗27. This is >=20 qubits. The authors explicitly state the circuit is shallow enough to be classically simulable via 2D tensor-network methods [37], so 27 qubits here does NOT imply classical intractability.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Partial / qualified. The paper does NOT empirically demonstrate a task where classical ML fails and simulable quantum wins. The only PROVEN classical-hard/quantum-easy separation is the reviewed DLOG/Z_p* construction of Liu et al. [16] (Appendix B.1): conditional on the classical hardness of discrete log, no classical learner beats 1/2+1/poly(n) while the covariant quantum kernel achieves near-perfect accuracy. That separation is super-polynomial-scaling and inherently NOT simulable with few qubits—it is a hardness-based asymptotic claim, not the 27-qubit experiment. The actual 27-qubit LCE experiment is an engineered benchmark explicitly chosen so it can be solved to zero error AND is acknowledged by the authors to be classically simulable (shallow, 2D topology), so it provides NO quantum advantage—it is a hardware/method validation, not an advantage demonstration. Caveats: advantage rests on the unproven DLOG hardness assumption; classical group-data kernels could plausibly also solve LCE; advantage disappears once circuits are deep/non-2D where simulation becomes hard but that regime is not tested. So under the hypothesis's "<=30-qubit simulable" lens, this paper does not show a win; it argues group-structured data is a promising candidate family.
DATASETS: Synthetic/engineered only. (1) LCE benchmark: two left-cosets C± = c±·S_graph of G=SU(2)^⊗27 with c± randomly drawn in [-π/2,π/2]^{2n}; each datum perturbed with Gaussian Euler-angle noise of variance ε=0.01; training set 10 points per label (20 total) for kernel alignment, test set 50 points per label (100 total). (2) Theoretical DLOG dataset of Liu et al. [16] over Z_p* (reviewed, not run): bit-strings labeled by discrete-log intervals. No real-world data.
CODE: none (no repository or framework linked in the read chunks; experiments on IBM Quantum hardware with SPSA-based kernel alignment).
RELEVANCE: High relevance to the hypothesis as a methodological and conceptual anchor: it defines a structured class (group/coset data) argued to be a natural fit for quantum advantage and connects to a proven hardness-based separation. But for the specific simulable (<=30-qubit) "quantum wins where classical fails" claim, its evidence is LOW—the 27-qubit demo is explicitly simulable and on a fabricated zero-error task with no classical baseline beaten.

### jerbi2023_beyond_kernel | 2023
TITLE: Quantum Machine Learning Beyond Kernel Methods
ONE_LINE: Unifies explicit, implicit (kernel), and data re-uploading quantum models as linear models in quantum feature spaces, and proves exponential resource separations (in qubits and data) showing that data re-uploading and explicit models can dramatically outperform quantum kernel methods, which overfit despite the representer theorem's training-loss guarantee.
QUBITS: Numerical simulations span n=2 to 12 qubits (no hardware; TensorFlow Quantum statevector simulation; no run uses >=20 qubits). Theoretical separations are stated asymptotically in d (qubits scale as Omega(d), unbounded). Data re-uploading construction for parities uses a single qubit.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Partial/qualified. The headline rigorous separations are between quantum model types (data re-uploading vs explicit/linear vs implicit kernel), not strictly quantum-vs-classical: the parity concept class used is explicitly noted to be classically easy (a depth-2 neural network learns it, and parities are solvable classically), so it does NOT demonstrate a quantum-over-classical advantage. The genuine quantum-vs-classical evidence is the empirical fashion-MNIST experiment (<=12 qubits, fully simulable), where explicit quantum models beat all tuned classical baselines from n=7 onward; however this is on a deliberately quantum-tailored/quantum-relabeled task (labels generated by a quantum explicit model with the Havlicek encoding conjectured-but-not-proven classically intractable), it is empirical not proven, advantage is in test MSE not a hardness result, and authors caution about trainability obstacles (barren plateaus for explicit, exponential kernel concentration for implicit) requiring exponentially many measurements. The paper's main rigorous classical-relevant message is a negative one about kernels: quantum (and classical) kernel methods provably need data linear in concept-class dimension (Omega(2^d)) and overfit despite regularization, dequantifying the apparent kernel advantage. No dequantization of the explicit-model advantage is shown, but no separation is proven to require quantum hardware either.
DATASETS: (1) Theoretical task: k-sparse parity functions g_A(x)=prod_{i in A} x_i on x in {-1,1}^d, with uniform and Daniely-Malach mixture input distributions; parametrized by input dimension d. (2) Numerical task: fashion-MNIST (28x28 grayscale clothing images) reduced via PCA to n-dimensional vectors (2<=n<=12), re-labeled by a quantum explicit model (Havlicek encoding + hardware-efficient ansatz, Pauli-Z label). Training set M=1000, validation 100, test 100; 10 random labeling functions per system size.
CODE: TensorFlow Quantum; code at https://github.com/sjerbi/QML-beyond-kernel (classical baselines via scikit-learn, ADAM optimizer).
RELEVANCE: High. Directly central to the hypothesis: it studies, both theoretically and via <=12-qubit simulation, tasks where quantum (especially data re-uploading and explicit) models outperform alternatives, and empirically shows a quantum-over-classical test-error gap on a simulable task. Importantly for the hypothesis, it is a sharp cautionary reference: it shows the parity separations are classically easy (so not a quantum advantage), shows quantum kernel methods overfit/require exponential data, and stresses that the favorable empirical task is quantum-tailored with only conjectured classical hardness and unresolved trainability caveats.

### schuld2021_data_encoding_fourier | 2021
TITLE: The effect of data encoding on the expressive power of variational quantum machine learning models
ONE_LINE: Shows that variational quantum models with Hamiltonian time-evolution data encoding can be written exactly as partial Fourier series whose accessible frequencies are set by the encoding-gate eigenvalues and whose coefficients are set by the rest of the circuit, yielding an asymptotic universal-function-approximation result.
QUBITS: No fixed/large qubit count; this is a theory paper. Demonstrations use a single qubit (Fig. 3) or r qubits for parallel Pauli encodings with r ∈ {1,3,5} (Fig. 4). Universality is an asymptotic statement requiring the number of subsystems m→∞ (m=K subsystems to reach degree K). No experiments at or above 20 qubits.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: No. The paper does NOT demonstrate any task where classical ML fails and quantum wins. Its central message is the opposite of a quantum-advantage claim: the learnable function class of these quantum models is exactly a (truncated) partial Fourier series, an entirely classical and—at the demonstrated scales—classically simulable/dequantizable object. The authors stress that expressivity is fundamentally capped by the data-encoding strategy even for circuits "which may even be classically intractable to simulate," and they explicitly warn that fair comparisons to classical models must feed the same classically pre-processed features to the classical model (Sec. IV A), implicitly cautioning against spurious advantage claims. The only universality result is (a) asymptotic (requires Hilbert-space dimension / subsystem count → ∞), (b) conditional on trainable blocks being arbitrary global unitaries that may need exponential depth, and (c) merely matches the classical universal-approximation property of Fourier series rather than exceeding any classical method. Whether such expressivity holds for constant/log/poly-depth trainable blocks is left as an open question. So relative to the hypothesis: the paper provides expressivity foundations but offers no evidence of classical-hard / quantum-easy separation, and indeed leans toward dequantization.
DATASETS: No real-world datasets. Purely synthetic target functions used in PennyLane simulations: degree-1 and degree-2 truncated Fourier series g(x)=Σ c_n e^{-nix} with c_0=0.1, c_1=c_2=0.15−0.15i (Fig. 3), and a degree-5 series g(x)=Σ_{n=-5}^{5} c_n e^{2inx} with c_n=0.05−0.05i, c_0=0 (Fig. 4). Sample/batch size 25, up to 200 Adam steps (lr 0.3).
CODE: Experiments performed with the PennyLane software library (Ref. [36]); no dedicated repository link given in the read chunks. Framework: PennyLane.
RELEVANCE: High relevance as a foundational/theory reference, low as direct evidence. It is a canonical paper for understanding what function classes 20+ qubit simulable variational models can express (partial Fourier series), which is essential for designing tasks and reasoning about expressivity/frequency matching. But it provides no empirical or proven classical-vs-quantum advantage, and its framing actually cautions against naive advantage claims, so it does not itself support the hypothesis that quantum wins where classical struggles.

### du2020_expressive_power_pqc | 2018 (arXiv:1810.11922; published Phys. Rev. Research 2, 033125, 2020)
TITLE: The Expressive Power of Parameterized Quantum Circuits
ONE_LINE: Proves that parameterized quantum circuits (MPQCs) have provably stronger expressive power than classical generative neural networks (DBM, RBM) and tensor-network PQCs for generative modeling, unless the polynomial hierarchy collapses, and introduces a post-selection-based Bayesian Quantum Circuit (BQC) for learning prior distributions.
QUBITS: All experiments are small simulations on Rigetti's pyQuil Quantum Virtual Machine (QVM): BAS 2x2 uses 7 qubits (4 data + 3 ancilla), BAS 3x3 uses 13 qubits (9 data + 4 ancilla), prior-learning uses 8 qubits (7 data + 1 ancilla). No hardware runs. No experiment reaches 20 qubits; the >=20-qubit regime is addressed only by the asymptotic poly(N) theory, not empirically.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Partially, and mostly theoretically. The strongest claim (Theorems 6-7) is a genuine separation: MPQCs can produce probability distributions that NO classical neural network (incl. DBM / long-range RBM) can sample efficiently and accurately unless the polynomial hierarchy collapses — a complexity-theoretic (conjectured-but-standard) hardness inherited from the IQP / Bremner-Jozsa-Shepherd result, not a learning/accuracy advantage on a concrete dataset. Important caveats: (1) The separation is about sampling expressivity, not about learning or generalization, and gives no efficient training guarantee. (2) The IQP-style hard instances are NOT in the <=30-qubit simulable regime in any demonstrated way — hardness is asymptotic; small instances (the 7-13 qubit BAS/prior tasks actually run) are trivially classically simulable, and on those the 'baselines' beaten are other quantum circuits (QCBM/DDQCL), not classical generative models. (3) The empirical BQC advantage (99.96% vs 98.46% BAS accuracy) is over other PQCs, so it does not evidence classical-vs-quantum separation. (4) Entanglement-entropy 'expressive power' is a representational proxy and the area/volume-law comparison is a known, somewhat coarse argument. (5) No dequantization is discussed, but the IQP hardness does not preclude classical approximate sampling for the specific easy tasks tested. Net: a proven expressivity separation in principle, but no demonstrated task where a 20+-qubit simulable quantum method empirically beats a trained classical model.
DATASETS: Synthetic/engineered only. (1) Bars-and-Stripes (BAS) images: 2x2 (N_BAS=6 valid images, 4 data qubits) and 3x3 (N_BAS=14, 9 data qubits). (2) A toy prior-learning task: data sampled from a 2-class mixture p(x,lambda)=p(lambda)p(x|lambda) with two Gaussians N1(mu=16,sigma=2), N2(mu=64,sigma=4) encoded into 7 qubits; target class priors p(lambda1)=0.70 and 0.85. No real-world datasets.
CODE: No public repository. Experiments implemented in Python via pyQuil on Rigetti Forest QVM; paper states "Python codes for our experiments are available upon request." Framework: pyQuil / Rigetti Forest.
RELEVANCE: Medium-high. Directly on-topic for the hypothesis as a foundational theoretical pillar: it gives one of the cleanest arguments that PQC generative models are strictly more expressive than classical neural generative models (RBM/DBM) under PH-non-collapse. However, relevance to the SPECIFIC hypothesis (simulable 20+-qubit quantum methods empirically beating classical ML) is limited because all separations are asymptotic/complexity-theoretic, all experiments are <=13 qubits, and empirical comparisons are quantum-vs-quantum rather than quantum-vs-classical. Useful for motivation and theory framing, not as direct evidence of a simulable empirical advantage.

### gao2022_quantum_generative | 2022 (arXiv 2101.08354, Jan 2021; published Phys. Rev. X 2022)
TITLE: Enhancing Generative Models via Quantum Correlations
ONE_LINE: Proves that quantum correlations (nonlocality and contextuality) give a minimal "basis-enhanced" quantum extension of Bayesian networks unconditionally greater expressive power than classical Bayesian networks/HMMs, and demonstrates the gap numerically on real ML datasets via classically-simulable tensor networks.
QUBITS: No large hardware run and no explicit >=20-qubit simulation count is reported. Constructions are 1D and classically simulable: the basis-enhanced 2-gram is a cluster/GHZ state where each variable = 2 qubits (so the n=16/23/57-variable datasets correspond to roughly 2n ~ 32/46/114 logical qubits worth of cluster state, but evaluated as MPS with bond dimension <=12, not as a qubit simulator). The genuinely quantum-hardware-requiring example (Theorem 3) is a 2D cluster state on a square lattice with n qubits; no specific qubit number is given and it was not simulated.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Partially, with important caveats. The paper gives UNCONDITIONAL proofs that classical k-gram and HMM models provably cannot match the basis-enhanced (quantum-correlation-inspired) models under a finite-KL error model — a real "classical struggle" result rooted in nonlocality/contextuality. However, the winning models in the proofs and ALL numerical experiments are 1D tensor networks (MPS/MPO) that are EFFICIENTLY CLASSICALLY SIMULABLE; the authors explicitly frame these as quantum-inspired CLASSICAL algorithms, so this is not a case of simulable-quantum beating all-classical — a classical computer runs both sides. The only example needing a real quantum computer is the 2D-Bayesian-network/cluster-state construction (Theorem 3), whose separation is CONDITIONAL (assumes the polynomial hierarchy does not collapse, multiplicative-error model) and is NOT demonstrated empirically. The finite-KL error model is also idealized (relies on exact-zero probability structure), and the empirical gap is modest (1–2.5 bits) and uses extra parameters (mitigated by the 5σ likelihood-ratio test). So: provable classical limitation in expressivity yes; a simulated >=20-qubit quantum model beating classical on a practical task with proven advantage — not shown.
DATASETS: Three real ML sequential datasets: biofam (family life-course sequences; length n=16, output alphabet M=8) used as a "local" control; Promoter Gene Sequences (DNA promoter vs non-promoter; n=57, M=4); SPECT Heart (binary feature vectors of heart images; n=23, M=2). A quarter of biofam/Promoter data was held out for testing; the standard SPECT test split was used. No image/NLP large-scale corpora; sizes are small UCI-style sequence datasets.
CODE: None explicitly linked in the read sections; custom Riemannian/unitary gradient-descent training of MPS/MPO tensor networks (method adapted from their Ref. [61]). No Qiskit/PennyLane/TFQ repo mentioned.
RELEVANCE: Medium. Highly relevant conceptually: it is one of the cleanest results identifying WHY quantum models can outdo classical generative models (nonlocality/contextuality) and shows an empirical expressivity gap on real datasets. But it is medium rather than high for the specific hypothesis because the demonstrated advantage uses classically-simulable 1D tensor networks (quantum-inspired classical methods), not 20+ qubit quantum simulation; the truly quantum-only advantage is 2D, conditional, and untested. Useful as a theoretical anchor and a cautionary example that many "quantum advantage" generative results dequantize to classical MPS.

### lloyd2020_quantum_embeddings | 2020
TITLE: Quantum embeddings for machine learning
ONE_LINE: Proposes "quantum metric learning": instead of training a variational measurement, train the quantum feature map (embedding) to maximally separate data classes in Hilbert space, after which the optimal classifying measurement is known in closed form (Helstrom for trace/l1 distance, fidelity/overlap for Hilbert-Schmidt/l2 distance).
QUBITS: Very small in experiments: 1-qubit and 2-qubit embeddings (simulated analytically in PennyLane). No experiment uses >=20 qubits. Larger numbers appear only in feasibility/illustrative discussion: a 2^50 ~ 10^15 dim embedding needing 50 controlled-SWAPs on 101 qubits; near-term hardware estimates cite ~100 superconducting or ion-trap qubits able to embed O(10^10)-O(10^11) classical bits within coherence time. SWAP test needs 2n+1 qubits; inversion test needs only n qubits.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: The paper does NOT demonstrate a task where classical methods fail and quantum wins. The authors explicitly state they "do not attempt to answer the question of whether quantum embeddings are better than classical machine learning methods" and that "the existence of a useful quantum advantage is an open question." The quantum-advantage argument is purely conjectural/complexity-theoretic: deep QAOA/IQP-style embeddings are argued to be classically intractable to reproduce "unless BQP=P", so the class of embeddings is strictly larger than classical ones. But (a) this is a statement about hardness of simulating the embedding, not a proven learning/generalization advantage; (b) it provides no task where classical ML underperforms; (c) the actual experiments use only 1-2 qubits, are trivially classically simulable (done analytically in PennyLane), and the hardest task (ants/bees) leans heavily on a classical ResNet doing most of the feature extraction. No dequantization is discussed, but the claimed intractability applies to >=~50-qubit, depth-O(n) circuits—well beyond the simulable 20-30 qubit regime of interest and beyond anything tested here. Relevance to the hypothesis is therefore conceptual scaffolding (a principled, resource-light framework) rather than evidence.
DATASETS: Toy/illustrative datasets only: (1) 2-D "moons" dataset (Fig. 2); (2) a synthetic non-overlapping but not linearly separable 1-D dataset embedded into a single qubit (Fig. 4); (3) an "ants vs bees" image classification task in a hybrid pipeline where images pass through a pre-trained ResNet whose 512-dim output is reduced by a trainable linear layer to a 2-D feature fed into the quantum circuit (Fig. 5). No dataset sizes/counts reported; batch sizes of 2-5 used.
CODE: No dedicated repo released; experiments implemented in the PennyLane hybrid-optimization framework (Bergholm et al., arXiv:1811.04968). Classifiers simulated analytically.
RELEVANCE: Medium. Highly relevant conceptually—it gives a clean, analytically grounded framework (metric learning in Hilbert space with known-optimal measurements) that is well suited to simulation and near-term devices, and motivates why quantum embeddings could access classifications classical models cannot. But it provides no empirical quantum-advantage evidence, no classical baselines, and uses only 1-2 qubits, so it does not directly support a "classical struggles, simulable quantum wins" claim at 20+ qubits.

### jerbi2023_shadows_qml | 2023 (v2 2024)
TITLE: Shadows of quantum machine learning
ONE_LINE: Introduces "shadow models" — quantum ML models trained on a quantum computer but deployed entirely classically via shadow tomography of a "flipped" linear model — and proves they retain a provable learning advantage over fully classical learners (under cryptographic assumptions) while being strictly weaker than fully quantum models.
QUBITS: Theoretical, parameter n (generic n-qubit / m=n+1-qubit constructions); no specific simulated or hardware qubit count is run. No experiments at 20+ qubits (or any qubit count) were performed — the paper is entirely analytical.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Yes, but only as a PROVABLE-IN-THEORY (conjectural-hardness) separation, not an empirical demonstration. The discrete-cube-root task (Theorem 1) is a genuine learning task where fully classical learners provably cannot beat random guessing while a shadow model succeeds — contingent on the DCR/RSA cryptographic hardness assumption (a widely-believed but unproven complexity conjecture, analogous to Liu-Arunachalam-Temme 2021). Important caveats: (1) The task is admittedly "somewhat contrived" — a cryptographic trapdoor construction, not a physically/practically motivated dataset; the authors flag finding physically-relevant analogues (e.g., ground-state properties) as future work. (2) The advantage relies on a quantum computer during TRAINING/shadowing (Shor's algorithm to find the trapdoor key), so it is NOT a dequantization — deployment is classical but the quantum step is essential. (3) The construction uses computational-basis states, so the "winning" quantum resource is Shor-style number-theoretic computation, not a many-qubit variational simulation; nothing about it requires or demonstrates the 20+-qubit simulation regime in the hypothesis. (4) No numbers, no simulation, no hardware. So the paper supports the hypothesis's existence-claim in principle but provides no simulable >=20-qubit empirical evidence.
DATASETS: None. The paper explicitly states "Data sharing is not applicable... as no datasets were generated or analyzed." Tasks are abstract/constructed: a discrete-cube-root learning task over Z_N (target g_s(x) keyed to RSA-like trapdoor) used as a theoretical separation, and a database-search toy model on x in {0,pi}^n used to separate flipped-shadowing from Fourier-shadowing.
CODE: None. No repository, no Qiskit/PennyLane/TFQ implementation; explicitly no data or code (theoretical paper).
RELEVANCE: Medium. Highly relevant conceptually — it rigorously establishes that a quantum-trained, classically-deployed model can solve a task classical ML provably cannot (supporting the "classical struggles, quantum wins" premise) and clarifies the BPP < BPP/qgenpoly < BQP landscape. But low on the specific operational angle of the hypothesis: there are no simulations, no 20+-qubit experiments, no datasets, and the advantage rests on a contrived cryptographic task with a Shor-based (not simulation-based) quantum step. Useful as a theoretical anchor/framing citation rather than an empirical demonstration.

### tang2019_dequantization_recommendation | 2019
TITLE: A quantum-inspired classical algorithm for recommendation systems
ONE_LINE: A classical ("quantum-inspired") sampling algorithm that solves the recommendation-systems task in time polylogarithmic in matrix dimensions, "dequantizing" the Kerenidis-Prakash quantum recommendation algorithm and proving it gives no exponential quantum speedup.
QUBITS: None. No quantum circuits are simulated or run; no qubit counts appear. The entire contribution is a classical algorithm. The referenced quantum algorithm (KP17b) is discussed only abstractly.
CLASSICAL_STRUGGLE: Strongly negative evidence for the hypothesis. This is the foundational "dequantization" result: it proves that a task previously believed to be one of the best candidates for an exponential QML speedup (Kerenidis-Prakash recommendation systems) can be solved classically in polylog(m,n) time, only polynomially slower than the quantum algorithm. The advantage is removed by a rigorous proof (Theorems 1 and 2), not merely empirically. Crucial condition/caveat: the classical algorithm requires l2-norm sampling access to the input, which Tang argues is the fair classical analogue of the quantum state-preparation assumption; under that matched assumption there is no exponential gap. Caveats on the classical side: large exponents/constants (from FKV), restrictive model assumptions (knowing a constant fraction of T, data held in a special structure) making it impractical, and poly(1/eps) rather than log(1/eps) error dependence. Net: this paper is direct counter-evidence to claims that quantum methods excel where classical methods struggle on this class of low-rank linear-algebra/recommendation tasks.
DATASETS: None. This is a purely theoretical complexity-theory / algorithms paper with proofs only; no experiments, no real or synthetic datasets, no numerical benchmarks. The data model is an abstract m x n preference matrix T assumed close to rank k, with a subsampled matrix A held in the data structure (recommendation model of Kumar et al. 1998 / Azar et al. 2001 / Drineas et al. 2002).
CODE: none (theoretical paper; no implementation, repository, or framework provided).
RELEVANCE: high - directly relevant as counter-evidence. It is the seminal dequantization paper showing that a flagship QML "exponential speedup" task is in fact efficiently classically solvable, sharpening any claim that quantum methods excel where classical ones fail and establishing the l2-norm-sampling framework needed to fairly compare QML and classical ML.
